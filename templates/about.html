How It Works

Cybersecurity Awareness Hub

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate legitimate businesses, financial institutions, or trusted individuals to trick victims into revealing sensitive information, such as passwords, social security numbers, and credit card details.

These attacks typically occur through emails, SMS messages (smishing), or phone calls (vishing), directing users to enter details on a fake website that looks almost identical to the genuine portal.

Common Types of Phishing

1. Spear Phishing

Targeted phishing campaigns customized for a specific individual or organization. Attackers gather personal details from social media or company directories to build highly convincing emails.

2. Whaling

A highly focused spear-phishing attack directed at high-profile executives (CEOs, CFOs, government officials) to steal corporate credentials, trade secrets, or initiate massive wire transfers.

3. Clone Phishing

Attackers clone a previously delivered, legitimate email containing an attachment or link. They replace the file/link with a malicious version and send it from a spoofed address claiming it is an update.

4. HTTPS Phishing

Attackers install free SSL certificates on their malicious domains to display the padlock icon in the address bar, tricking users into believing the site is safe. (HTTPS only encrypts the connection; it does not guarantee the site's legitimacy).

Defense & Prevention Methods

Preventing phishing requires a combination of modern security tools, web filters, and human awareness. Follow these guidelines to stay protected:

  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) MFA adds an extra layer of defense. Even if an attacker steals your password, they cannot gain access without your secondary verification token.
  • Inspect URLs Thoroughly Always look closely at domain names for typos (e.g., paypa1.com instead of paypal.com), subdomains (e.g., paypal.verify-secure.com), and unexpected extensions.
  • Never Click Direct Login Links If an email claims your account is locked, do not click the link inside. Open your browser and navigate directly to the official website bookmark.
  • Use Web Protection Software Keep your browsers updated and enable built-in safe browsing tools. Utilize phishing detection utilities (like this portal) to scan suspicious links beforehand.
Quick Warning Signs
High Urgency

Emails demanding immediate action ("Your account will be suspended in 24 hours") are designed to provoke panic.

Suspicious Domain

Domains that don't match the sender's organization, or use free hosting providers (e.g., 000webhostapp.com).

Generic Greetings

Salutations like "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name imply bulk generic phishing spam.

Be Alert, Stay Safe!

Cybersecurity is a shared responsibility. Educate your peers and report suspicious domains to reporting agencies like Google Safe Browsing or APWG.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about PhishGuard.

PhishGuard is an AI-powered phishing website detector built using Python, Flask, and Machine Learning heuristics. It analyzes URL structure to determine whether a website is safe, suspicious, or dangerous.
The scanner analyzes 10+ URL parameters including: HTTPS availability, URL length, IP address usage, suspicious keywords, URL shorteners, subdomain count, domain hyphenation, TLD reputation, special characters, and the presence of "@" symbols. Each parameter is weighted and combined into a 0–100% risk score.
Scanned URLs are stored locally in the application's scan history (a JSON file on the server). No data is sent to any third-party service. You can view, search, and delete your scan history at any time from the History page.
0–30% = Safe: The URL has few or no phishing indicators.
31–70% = Suspicious: Some risk factors present; proceed with caution.
71–100% = Dangerous: Multiple phishing indicators detected; avoid this website.
PhishGuard uses heuristic-based ML analysis which is highly effective but not 100% perfect. Some sophisticated phishing sites may bypass certain checks (e.g., those using HTTPS). Always exercise caution and combine PhishGuard with other security measures.
